# First Vertical Slice

## Product Call

The first real vertical slice should not start in a menu and then cut to a different game.

It should start as a living side-scroller screen that becomes playable with no hard transition.

That gives `Killer Queen` a stronger identity immediately:

- title screen and game world are the same place
- pressing start feels like the world waking up, not a screen swap
- the player meets the tone, movement, and weapon fantasy in the first minute

## Opening Thesis

The game opens on a ruined sky dock at dusk.

The camera is already live.
The skyline already moves.
The ship rails, banners, deck lights, and distant hive breach are already visible.

On the title state, the player sees:

- the dock
- the resistance skiff moored at center-right
- the quiet deck where the run will begin
- a restrained `Press Start` prompt

When the player presses start, the same screen becomes the intro:

1. an inbound pod or skiff crosses the background
2. the hero lands hard onto the dock deck
3. the HUD fades in while control unlocks
4. the player runs right into the ship armory alcove
5. they choose their first starter weapon there
6. the first playable side-scroller stretch begins immediately

No fade to black.
No scene-swap feeling.
No dead setup menu before the game actually starts.

## First 10 Minutes

The first vertical slice should cover one tight sequence:

1. `Sky Dock Title State`
- title screen is the live opening room
- no combat yet
- sets tone and silhouette

2. `Landing Stretch`
- short side-scrolling tutorial lane
- teaches move, jump, burst, and attack
- one clean recovery moment

3. `Armory Choice`
- choose `Arc Cutter` or `Blaster`
- the unchosen weapon stays visible in the ship
- player can earn it later; this is a starter identity choice, not a permanent class lock

4. `Dock Breach Run`
- first real combat strip
- introduces mites, one crawler, one pod
- proves the chosen starter feels weak but usable

5. `Sanctuary Unlock`
- the player reaches the first safe hub pocket
- from here the game opens into the small hub and the first stage door

This is enough to prove:

- the zero-transition start
- the tone
- the weapon choice
- the first weak upgrade loop
- the path from intro to hub to first stage

## Starter Weapon Choice

The player chooses one starter at the ship armory.

Rule:

- the starter should feel a little underpowered but full of potential
- the choice should change the first hour
- the game should later give access to both weapons
- the early choice is about rhythm and problem-solving style, not permanent exclusion

### Arc Cutter Starter

Working fantasy:
- close-range pressure
- better kill pop
- riskier opening

Starting weakness:
- short reach
- limited string
- no big lane-clearing move yet

### Blaster Starter

Working fantasy:
- safe openers
- weak-point checks
- cleaner pod and mite handling early

Starting weakness:
- low damage
- weak crowd pressure
- less momentum payout than the cutter

## Humble Upgrade Tracks

These are intentionally small, readable, and shippable.

They should not be percentage sludge.
Each one should give the player one obvious new comfort or one obvious new trick.

## Arc Cutter Early Track

Starter frame:
- `Service Fang`
- 2-hit ground string
- weak air cut
- very short reach
- no dash slash

Upgrade 1:
- `Sharpened Edge`
- adds the third ground hit
- slightly cleans up hitbox width
- player finally feels like they can finish a crawler cleanly

Upgrade 2:
- `Lift Notch`
- unlocks a modest Rising Slash
- not a huge launcher, just enough to answer mites above head height
- teaches the player that the cutter owns vertical confirms

Upgrade 3:
- `Rebound Spring`
- blade kills now grant a clearer pop and a tiny burst refund
- this is the first upgrade that makes the cutter feel stylish instead of merely functional

Upgrade 4:
- `Dash Motor`
- unlocks a narrow Dash Slash
- not the late-game big sweep, just a committed straight-line cut
- this is where the weapon starts feeling like the real fantasy

## Blaster Early Track

Starter frame:
- `Dock Blaster`
- single straight shot
- decent safety
- poor kill speed
- weak against armored enemies unless the weak point is exposed

Upgrade 1:
- `Refit Barrel`
- shot speed and contact feel improve
- better at clipping mites and pods before they clutter the route
- does not increase spectacle, just reliability

Upgrade 2:
- `Twin Feed`
- each attack becomes a restrained 2-round burst
- makes the weapon feel less sad without turning it into a spray gun

Upgrade 3:
- `Target Coil`
- light aim correction against mites, pod cores, and exposed crawler vents
- keeps the gun readable and useful without becoming auto-play

Upgrade 4:
- `Pressure Spike`
- repeated hits on the same target trigger a small stagger pop
- this is the first upgrade that makes the gun feel smart instead of merely safe

## Why Weak Upgrades Matter

The early weapon game should feel like:

`barely enough -> noticeably better -> finally expressive`

That arc matters because:

- it makes the ship choice feel meaningful
- it gives the first missions a tangible improvement loop
- it makes later stylish forms feel earned

Do not start with flashy overdesigned movesets.
Let the player climb into the fantasy.

## First Stage Pairing

The first vertical slice should include:

- `1` intro side-scroller path
- `1` compact sanctuary hub sector
- `1` first stage door: `Hive Shaft`
- `3` missions only

Recommended missions:

1. `Breach`
- finish the opening route and reach the safe chamber

2. `Nestbreaker`
- destroy marked brood pods in the early Hive Shaft layout
- best mission for proving the weapon choice

3. `Summit`
- climb to the first shaft crown under mild pressure
- best mission for proving movement identity

4. `Rival Boss`
- the first real boss uses an elite overclocked version of the weapon the player did not choose
- this is the Pokemon-starter check in action form
- beating this boss is how the player earns the alternate weapon back in a damaged starter state

## First Boss Rule

The first boss should not be a neutral monster.

It should be a `starter rival boss`.

That means:

- if the player chose `Arc Cutter`, the boss uses a terrifying overclocked `Blaster`
- if the player chose `Blaster`, the boss uses a terrifying overclocked `Arc Cutter`

The point is product clarity:

- the player sees the ceiling of the weapon they did not choose
- the player feels temporary envy and pressure
- the game says clearly that both weapons matter
- the reward for winning is not the full boss version, but the damaged starter version of that alternate weapon

This is the cleanest way to preserve the starter choice while still converting the game into a real dual-weapon action game early.

## Rival Boss Variants

### If Player Chose Arc Cutter

Boss loadout:
- `Maximum Blaster`
- higher fire rate
- wider lane denial
- stronger pod/mite suppression
- cleaner anti-air coverage

Player lesson:
- gun is not the boring option
- ranged control can look terrifying when pushed hard
- their cutter choice is still cooler up close, but not universally superior

Reward after win:
- the player earns the damaged `Dock Blaster` starter frame, not the maximum version

### If Player Chose Blaster

Boss loadout:
- `Maximum Arc Cutter`
- long committed dash slashes
- stronger rebound chains
- brutal close-range punish windows
- aggressive vertical confirms

Player lesson:
- blade is not just reckless flavor
- the cutter can become the stylish centerpiece of the game
- their blaster choice is still useful, but not the whole combat answer

Reward after win:
- the player earns the damaged `Service Fang` starter frame, not the maximum version

## Reward Rule

Do not give the alternate weapon back immediately after the armory choice.

Do not wait too long either.

The right timing is:

- after the first `Hive Shaft` mission trio
- after the rival boss
- as a recovery/salvage reward from the boss fight aftermath

That keeps the starter choice meaningful long enough to matter, then opens the game into its real dual-weapon identity.

## Enemy Pairing In The Intro

Use only these in the first vertical slice:

- `Mite Drifter`
- `Shellback Crawler`
- `Brood Pod`
- optional `Pulse Mine` as a support hazard, not a headline enemy

Why:

- mites test vertical response
- crawlers test weapon contrast
- pods test route control

That is enough to prove the combat identity.

## Hub Integration

The first sanctuary hub sector should include:

- the landed ship
- the armory where the first choice happened
- one NPC or terminal that explains upgrades plainly
- one visible but locked route that promises future progression
- one stage door into `Hive Shaft`

The hub should feel like:

- a safe pocket
- a visual reward
- a reminder that the game will open outward after the panic intro

## UI Rules

- title state UI must be minimal
- `Press Start` should feel diegetic and low-noise
- once gameplay begins, HUD fades in rather than appearing all at once
- the weapon choice screen should be an in-world armory interaction, not a modal menu slab
- upgrade reads should be short and concrete

Good upgrade copy:

- `Adds a third cut.`
- `Unlocks a short rising slash.`
- `Shots fire in a two-round burst.`
- `Blade kills refund a little burst fuel.`

Bad upgrade copy:

- `Increases offensive efficiency by 12%.`
- `Enhances vertical engagement.`

## Not In This Slice

- all six stages
- late-game weapon modules
- multiple hub sectors
- deep narrative exposition
- branching mission map UI
- more than one real upgrade tier for the unchosen weapon

## Acceptance Criteria

The first vertical slice is correct if:

- the title screen and intro feel like one continuous place
- the player understands their first weapon choice without a tutorial dump
- both starters feel weak but viable
- each upgrade is noticeable in one run
- the first three enemies prove different weapon jobs cleanly
- the player reaches a real sanctuary and sees the larger game promise

## Build Order

1. side-scroller title/start scene
2. armory choice interaction
3. starter weapon state and weak-upgrade data
4. first sanctuary hub pocket
5. first `Hive Shaft` mission trio

Do not build broad progression UI before this slice feels good.
