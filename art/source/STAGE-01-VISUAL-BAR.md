# Stage 01 Visual Bar

This is the quality bar for the first real playable chapter of `Killer Queen`.

Stage 1 is not a neutral tutorial.
It is not a precision platform exam.
It is not a pure jetpack joyride.

It is a `combat showcase with jetpack flow between fights`.

The player should feel:

```text
land
    -> move clean
    -> hit first enemies hard
    -> correct a line with the jetpack
    -> keep momentum
    -> push into a darker cave
    -> face the first real boss
```

If the stage stops feeling like that, it is off-bar.

## Product Read

Stage 1 should sell four things at once:

- the hero is stylish immediately
- both starter weapons are viable and fun
- the jetpack is a cool movement amplifier, not a crutch
- the world is dark, heavy, and memorable without sacrificing clarity

The visual promise is:

`Capcom 16-bit action game with bug-royalty menace, dark dusk staging, and clean arcade readability`

## Stage 1 Role

The first chapter exists to prove:

1. the hero is fun to control
2. the combat is worth learning
3. the jetpack makes fights and recoveries look cooler
4. the world has authored drama

That means the stage structure should be:

- short calm setup
- fast first combat beat
- short mobility lane
- stronger combat cluster
- darker cave approach
- boss foyer
- boss

Do not build the whole stage as one long uninterrupted climb.
It needs beats, not just height.

## Non-Negotiable Screen Hierarchy

Every moment in Stage 1 must have one dominant read.

Priority order:

1. player
2. immediate threat or interactable
3. walkable surface
4. landmark or route cue
5. background atmosphere

If a background prop beats the player in contrast, cut it back.
If UI beats the boss during the boss, cut it back.
If effects beat collision readability, cut them back.

## Beat-By-Beat Hierarchy

### 1. Title Attract

Dominant read:
- ship silhouette
- hero silhouette

Secondary:
- restrained command list

Everything else should support those two reads.
The menu must feel like an overlay on a living world, not a UI wall.

### 2. Landing / Starter Claim

Dominant read:
- hero entering the deck
- weapon rack currently in focus

Secondary:
- bottom armory prompt

The claim should feel ceremonial:
- bright rack pulse
- one clear badge stamp
- one clean camera emphasis

No duplicate information blocks.

### 3. Early Combat Run

Dominant read:
- player
- nearest threat lane

Secondary:
- next platform or refuel path

The player should never have to search the screen to know:
- what can hit them
- where they can land
- where the next forward line is

### 4. Cave Approach

Dominant read:
- cave mouth silhouette
- hero path into it

Secondary:
- darker surrounding structure

The cave entrance must read like a destination, not just another dark slab.

### 5. Boss Foyer / Boss Fight

Dominant read:
- boss silhouette
- player silhouette

Secondary:
- floor, walls, weak-state read

Everything else gets quieter.
This is where prototype clutter dies.

## Stage 1 Shape Language

The first stage should not be built from repeated anonymous bars.

Use these forms:

- broad deck planes at the start
- suspended service platforms with obvious support language
- broken transit braces
- insect-rib cave framing
- one or two monumental landmark shapes only

Avoid:

- repeated flat rectangles with no material identity
- random side pillars that do nothing compositionally
- generic cave-black ceilings
- visual clutter that makes the next route feel hidden

Each section of the stage should have one memorable silhouette:

- the dock ship
- the armory rails
- the first large combat lane
- the cave mouth
- the boss chamber arch

If a section has no signature shape, redesign it.

## Player Silhouette Rule

The player should read as `hybrid kit, blade-led identity`.

That means:

- the blade side is the stronger side read
- the gun is visible, but compact
- the jet nozzles are always part of the silhouette language
- the head and boots remain stable anchors in every pose

Hierarchy inside the player silhouette:

1. head / visor
2. torso block
3. blade-side arm read
4. boots
5. jetpack nozzles
6. gun module

The player must not read like:
- a generic shooter
- a tiny dual-weapon toy
- a neutral platformer pawn

The player should read like:
- a fast blade-user who also carries a ranged answer

## First-Hour Pose Rules

In the first hour, the player should spend most of their time in these readable states:

- run with chest-forward commitment
- burst with a long clean travel line
- slash with a strong forward contact frame
- shoot with a compact blaster extension, not a giant rifle pose
- recover with controlled momentum, not floppy reset frames

The gun should not dominate idle or run poses.
The blade does not need to be oversized, but it must be the stronger identity cue.

## Weapon Read Rules

### Blade

The blade should communicate:

- commitment
- rebound
- aggression

Visual traits:

- brighter edge read than torso
- strong contact arc
- clean anticipation and follow-through
- no giant anime smear

### Gun

The gun should communicate:

- lane clear
- precision
- support fire

Visual traits:

- compact module
- clear muzzle beat
- readable projectile line
- no oversized sci-fi rifle silhouette

Both weapons must look viable.
The blade just gets priority in the character identity.

## Darkness Rule

Stage 1 can be dark and oppressive.
It cannot be visually flat.

This is `designed darkness`, not generic blackness.

Use darkness to create:

- mood
- depth
- tunnel vision toward action
- boss drama

Do not use darkness to:

- hide route reads
- flatten silhouettes
- excuse weak staging

## Stage 1 Palette

Stage 1 should use a controlled dusk-hive palette:

- `Abyss Ink` `#090b10`
- `Bruised Violet` `#241a34`
- `Dead Steel` `#33404d`
- `Cold Slate` `#556575`
- `Burnt Amber` `#d88b39`
- `Signal Rust` `#a84f2f`
- `Surgical Teal` `#42bfc0`
- `Bone Flare` `#e6d7b4`
- `Visor White` `#f2eee0`

Usage:

- abyss ink and bruised violet carry the distant darkness
- dead steel and cold slate define structure
- burnt amber marks danger and machinery heat
- surgical teal marks live tech sparingly
- bone flare and visor white are reserved for key highlights

Do not invent extra accent colors for Stage 1.

## Reserved Value Bands

These are brightness bands, not hard laws, but they must hold visually.

### Background Sky / Void
- `5-18%` value
- soft dusk transitions only
- no bright focal accents

### Distant Background Structures
- `8-24%` value
- large silhouette masses only
- details simplified and quiet

### Midground Props
- `14-32%` value
- readable shapes, but clearly subordinate to gameplay

### Walkable Surfaces
- `24-46%` value
- top edges must be clearly readable
- undersides darker and quieter

### Hazards / Threat Cores
- `48-78%` value
- brightest accents reserved for active danger, weak points, and hit windows

### Player
- `52-90%` value
- player keeps the highest controlled contrast on the gameplay plane
- visor, blade edge, and boots carry the strongest readability anchors

If the background climbs into the player band, it is wrong.

## Color Reservation Rules

- Amber is for danger, heat, active rails, and warning seams.
- Teal is for live tech, prompts, and limited energy reads.
- Bone and visor white are for hero highlights, boss highlights, and impact peaks only.
- Do not use full-intensity teal and amber everywhere at once.

The moment every prop glows, nothing matters.

## Platform Rules For Stage 1

Platforms should feel authored and premium, not debug-functional.

Each platform family must answer:

- what is it made of
- why is it here
- how does it support movement rhythm

Stage 1 platform families:

1. dock steel
- broad stable surfaces
- clear thick top edge

2. service rails
- slimmer suspended traversal pieces
- visual support struts

3. fuel pads
- bright but controlled refuel identity
- should read valuable, not decorative

4. spring plates
- obvious mechanical compression language
- should look punchy before activation

5. cave catwalks
- darker, tighter, more dangerous
- still clearly walkable

## Combat Read Rules

During combat, the eye should be able to parse:

- player
- active enemy
- incoming danger
- next safe landing line

in under one second.

That means:

- enemy clusters must be staged, not piled
- projectiles need clear trajectories
- hit sparks should be brief and directional
- slash arcs should not cover feet or platform edges
- damage flashes should describe contact, not wash out the sprite

## Boss Chamber Rules

The first boss room must feel like an event.

What the room needs:

- one dominant arch or chamber silhouette
- one strong boss backlight zone
- quieter side geometry
- a readable floor line
- enough negative space around the rival for the pose to read

What it must not do:

- bury the boss in darkness
- let UI eat the frame
- use the same values as the player
- confuse decoration with arena boundaries

## Motion Rules

Jetpack motion should read like:

- carry
- correction
- swagger

not:

- desperation hover
- technical precision tax

So the visual motion language should favor:

- broad forward movement lines
- clean diagonal burst paths
- obvious recovery pops after kills
- brief readable exhaust beats

Do not overfill the screen with exhaust clutter.

## Forbidden Moves

- repeated anonymous bars for most of the stage
- all-black caves with amber dots
- UI panels that dominate action beats
- player silhouette that reads gun-first
- effects that are brighter than the gameplay problem they explain
- decorative glows on non-interactive props
- background contrast that climbs into the player band
- “retro grime” noise used to fake detail

## Approval Test

Stage 1 is on-bar if:

- one screenshot tells you where you are instantly
- the player is always the strongest gameplay read
- both weapons look usable and desirable
- the jetpack looks cool without becoming mush
- the cave boss room feels like a designed set piece, not a dark rectangle

If any of those fail, simplify before adding more art.
